# Audio Direction

Status: Approved

## Purpose

This document defines the current audio identity for Irys+Ion.

Audio must support:

- intense combat;
- readable Techniques;
- the ancient-to-awake environmental progression;
- the distinction between Irys, Ion, and the planetary AI;
- sparse narrative delivery;
- the emotional escalation into the final confrontation.

The soundtrack and sound design should feel authored as part of the same world rather than layered onto the game afterward.

## Musical Identity

The primary musical language is high-intensity industrial electronic music.

Core materials may include:

- mechanical percussion;
- breakbeats;
- synthetic bass;
- machine pulses;
- processed metallic rhythm;
- restrained melodic motifs;
- drones;
- distorted electrical texture;
- synchronized production-system sounds.

The score should not settle into generic retro synthwave.

It should feel:

- mechanical;
- physical;
- urgent;
- modern;
- hostile;
- precise.

Music supports combat intensity without overwhelming Technique readability or enemy telegraphs.

## Continuous Soundtrack

Gameplay should always retain some form of musical soundtrack.

Intensity may vary substantially, but ordinary gameplay should not fall into complete musical silence.

Suitable low-intensity states include:

- sparse harmonic beds;
- distant rhythmic pulses;
- sustained synthetic texture;
- restrained motif fragments;
- degraded musical ambience;
- minimal tonal machinery.

Complete silence is reserved for brief authored dramatic moments.

Suitable uses include:

- a critical beat during the planetary-AI transfer;
- the instant before the planetary-AI blade attempts to escape;
- another similarly deliberate narrative interruption.

The requirement for continuous soundtrack does not mean every room must carry dense or high-intensity music.

Music must continue to yield space to:

- enemy telegraphs;
- Technique identity;
- dialogue tones;
- environmental machinery;
- major narrative sound effects.

## Recurring Melodic Hook

A single recognizable melodic hook should recur throughout the game.

It may appear as:

- a sparse synth line;
- an Ark harmonic sequence;
- a machine pulse;
- a boss variation;
- a dialogue-associated interval;
- an environmental tone;
- a transformed orchestral phrase.

The hook should initially feel like part of the game’s musical language rather than an overt anthem.

Its emotional meaning becomes clearest during the final fight.

The hook provides continuity across otherwise contrasting stage and boss treatments.

## Environmental Score Progression

### Ark

The Ark uses warmer synthetic harmony and controlled electronic texture.

Its music should feel:

- protected;
- deliberate;
- intimate despite the large pod field;
- technologically alive;
- distinct from the planetary megastructure.

The recurring melodic hook may appear here in its warmest early form.

### Industrial Perimeter

Early megastructure music emphasizes:

- distant machinery;
- low drones;
- sparse percussion;
- empty scale;
- long mechanical decay;
- restrained active rhythm.

The player should feel that the structure is ancient and incomprehensibly large.

### Adaptive Infrastructure

Rhythm becomes clearer and more responsive.

Add:

- articulated percussion;
- mechanical syncopation;
- active pulse layers;
- denser movement;
- sounds suggesting systems observing and repositioning.

### Experimentation Region

The score becomes more synthetic, deliberate, and uncomfortable without relying on horror clichés.

Use:

- altered human-adjacent tonal textures;
- restrained dissonance;
- unstable harmonic doubling;
- more articulated rhythmic movement.

### Control Center

The control center should sound fully awake, maintained, precise, and computational.

Its music may be brighter and denser than previous regions rather than simply darker.

Use:

- synchronized machine rhythm;
- clean high-frequency detail;
- ultraviolet-feeling synthetic timbre;
- controlled intensity;
- minimal sonic decay;
- strong forward momentum.

### Shutdown Return

After the planetary AI transfers into its blade-form, the facility loses its coordinated sonic identity.

The return route should remove or degrade:

- precise pulses;
- stable rhythmic synchronization;
- planetary-AI tonal signatures;
- coordinated machinery layers.

Replace them with:

- failing mechanisms;
- irregular motor decay;
- unsynchronized impacts;
- distant shutdown events;
- unstable environmental rhythm;
- exposed structural ambience.

The score remains present but may thin to a depleted, unstable musical bed so the player hears that the intelligence has left the building without the game losing its continuous soundtrack.

## Reactive Stage Layering

Each ordinary stage uses one continuous musical identity.

Exploration, tension, and combat are layered states of the same underlying track rather than unrelated tracks that restart when an encounter begins.

Enemy engagement may introduce or intensify:

- percussion;
- synthetic bass;
- rhythmic machinery;
- harmonic pressure;
- high-frequency motion;
- additional motif fragments.

When combat ends, those layers should recede gradually rather than stopping through an abrupt track reset.

The system should preserve musical continuity across the stage.

Boss encounters retain separate fully authored tracks.

The reactive system should remain restrained.

It does not need to respond independently to:

- every Technique;
- every hit;
- every enemy action;
- every generated body trait;
- moment-to-moment combo performance.

A small number of clear authored music states is preferable to a fragile highly granular adaptive score.

## Boss Music

Boss tracks should be focused and highly rhythmic.

They should reinforce:

- intensity;
- phase escalation;
- attack cadence;
- boss identity.

Boss music must not literally announce every attack.

The player should read attacks through visual and sound-effect telegraphs rather than memorizing a musical sequence.

Each boss track may transform the recurring melodic hook according to the boss’s place in the planetary AI’s development.

### Command Mech

The knight-like command mech should receive a convincing false-final track.

It should sound:

- imposing;
- complete;
- controlled;
- technologically triumphant;
- more formally structured than earlier boss music.

The track must plausibly feel like the campaign’s climax until the Anti-Irys transfer occurs.

### Anti-Irys Final Battle

The Anti-Irys battle breaks sharply from the predominantly industrial electronic score.

The final fight uses a dramatic, epic, classically orchestral treatment of the recurring melodic hook.

The orchestral language dominates.

Possible materials include:

- strings;
- brass;
- orchestral percussion;
- restrained choir;
- limited electronic connective texture.

The contrast should make the final duel feel singular and emotionally climactic without sounding disconnected from the rest of the score.

A suitable three-part structure is:

1. Anti-Irys arrival: controlled, severe, elegant orchestration.
2. Ark intervention: the recurring hook becomes clearer and more emotional as the pod chamber transforms.
3. Final phase: the fullest orchestral statement of the hook as Irys and Ion’s roles converge.

When the planetary-AI blade attempts to escape, the score should strip down sharply.

Ion’s killing blow may leave the melodic hook exposed through one instrument, one voice-like synthetic tone, or another minimal statement before the ending begins.

## Dialogue Presentation

Dialogue is not conventionally voiced.

Text appears in dialogue boxes while modulated electrical tones imply speech.

The tones must communicate:

- speaker identity;
- cadence;
- mood;
- interruption;
- emphasis;
- presence.

They must not become intelligible synthesized speech.

Dialogue sounds should remain brief and unobtrusive enough that repeated runs do not become exhausting.

## Speaker Signatures

### Irys / Ark

Irys uses a warm, layered synthetic voice signature.

It may include:

- controlled harmonic motion;
- softly layered tones;
- stable warm intervals;
- subtle environmental resonance from the Ark itself.

The behavior of Ark machinery, pods, barriers, and lighting should also contribute to Irys’s audible presence.

### Ion

Ion uses a clear, narrow, resonant electrical signature.

It should feel:

- focused;
- immediate;
- portable;
- distinct from the Ark’s larger environmental resonance.

Ion’s tone should remain recognizable across every Irys body and every run.

### Planetary AI

The planetary AI uses a cold multiphonic signal.

It should feel:

- unnaturally stable;
- spatially diffuse;
- computational;
- invasive;
- certain rather than angry.

Its voice may appear to come from the surrounding facility before the transfer.

After the transfer, the same identity becomes localized to the planetary-AI blade and Anti-Irys.

### Anti-Irys

Anti-Irys does not possess a separate independent voice identity.

The planetary AI speaks through the Anti-Irys configuration.

The sound should make clear that the body is the wielder and the blade is the continuing intelligence.

## Irys–Ion Harmonic Relationship

Ion and Irys must sound clearly different from one another.

Ion uses a narrow, focused, immediate electrical signature.

Irys and the Ark use a warmer, layered, spatially larger signature.

Their identities should nevertheless derive from a subtle shared harmonic foundation.

That relationship may use:

- the same small interval;
- related overtone structures;
- a shared melodic fragment;
- complementary harmonic motion;
- differently voiced forms of the recurring hook.

The connection should remain subconscious through most of the game.

It must not function as an obvious early disclosure of the ending.

During the ending, the two signatures align completely for the first time.

The recurring melodic hook may then reveal that both identities were derived from the same musical material.

## Technique Sound Hierarchy

Every Technique requires an immediately recognizable sonic identity.

Technique sounds must communicate direction and commitment before decorative detail.

### Advance

Advance should emphasize:

- forward acceleration;
- linear cutting motion;
- rapid spatial displacement;
- a clear directional pass.

### Ascend

Ascend should emphasize:

- upward launch;
- rising tonal motion;
- lift and release;
- a clean vertical trajectory.

### Descend

Descend should emphasize:

- immediate downward snap;
- increasing force;
- compressed air or energy;
- terrain or target impact.

### Prime Armed State

The armed Prime state requires a persistent but restrained audible layer.

It must:

- remain recognizable while active;
- avoid becoming irritating during extended holds;
- distinguish activation from expiry;
- support Ion’s steady visual cue.

### Primed Techniques

Primed Techniques use:

- greater density;
- stronger low-frequency content;
- expanded spectral range;
- longer residue;
- more forceful commitment cues.

They must not simply be louder versions of Normal Techniques.

## Technique Tonal Relationship

Technique sounds may use restrained pitch contours related to the recurring melodic material.

A suitable initial attempt is:

- Advance: compact forward-driving or lightly rising contour;
- Ascend: clearer upward interval or rising spectral motion;
- Descend: falling, compressing, or weight-gathering contour;
- Prime: unresolved harmonic tension;
- Primed Technique: resolution or expansion of the relevant Technique identity.

This is an implementation experiment rather than a rigid requirement.

Technique sound must remain primarily:

- physical;
- mechanical;
- directional;
- readable;
- immediate.

The musical relationship should be subconscious.

If the result sounds gimmicky, excessively melodic, or reduces combat clarity, Sol should reduce the relationship to shared harmonics or remove it.

Technique recognition must not depend on the tonal experiment succeeding.

## Contact and Damage

Successful player contact should read primarily on the enemy.

Possible materials include:

- synthetic fracture;
- metallic shear;
- electrical discharge;
- armor separation;
- component resonance;
- short impact transients.

Irys damage should remain mechanically dry.

Use:

- plate fracture;
- sparks;
- disrupted internal energy;
- structural failure;
- brief signal distortion.

Avoid wet gore or fluid sounds.

No sound effect should imply conventional blood or organic tissue damage.

## Generated-Body Sound Variation

Ion supplies the persistent recognizable core sound of every Technique.

The current Irys substrate may add restrained secondary variation through:

- body weight;
- joint texture;
- mechanical resonance;
- landing force;
- structural density;
- limb configuration;
- asymmetry;
- damaged or defective components.

Examples include:

- heavier frames carrying slightly denser or lower mechanical resonance;
- lightweight frames using sharper and more agile movement texture;
- asymmetric or defective frames producing subtle irregular actuator behavior;
- reinforced bodies producing firmer landing and impact support.

These differences should not be exaggerated.

Generated-body variation must never obscure or replace the persistent identity of:

- Advance;
- Ascend;
- Descend;
- Prime;
- Ion.

Not every body statistic requires an audible variation.

Use body-dependent audio only where it contributes meaningful physical character.

## Synthetic Exertion

Irys substrates may produce restrained feminine synthetic exertion and reaction sounds.

These sounds should be:

- muted;
- brief;
- infrequent;
- clearly artificial;
- subordinate to combat information.

Suitable materials include:

- breath-like actuator release;
- mechanical pressure venting;
- soft electrical strain;
- controlled vocal-adjacent tones;
- brief synthetic stress resonance.

Suitable moments include:

- receiving significant damage;
- a hard landing;
- exceptional physical strain;
- heavy Primed commitment;
- major authored displacement.

Avoid:

- constant grunting;
- ordinary human exertion libraries;
- frequent vocal repetition;
- exaggerated sexualized combat vocalization;
- sounds implying that each disposable substrate contains a separate persistent personality.

## Enemy and Boss Telegraphs

Enemy and boss attacks require consistent telegraph families.

Telegraphs must remain audible through music without becoming excessively loud.

Use distinctions in:

- pitch contour;
- rhythm;
- spatial position;
- attack envelope;
- timbre;
- repetition.

Late enemies may use refined versions of early lineage sounds so their ancestry remains audible.

Anti-Irys should use sounds related to the player’s Language without becoming indistinguishable from the player.

The planetary-AI blade must retain its own cold spectral identity.

## Boss Vocalization

Bosses 1, 2, and 4 do not use human-adjacent voices.

They communicate through distinct authored combinations of:

- mechanical calls;
- alarms;
- actuator stress;
- resonant structures;
- power-system tones;
- armor movement;
- weapon charging.

The restrained proto-Irys boss may produce sparse almost-human synthetic vocalizations.

Those sounds should feel:

- fragmentary;
- involuntary;
- incomplete;
- synthetic;
- unsettling without becoming grotesque.

The proto-Irys must not deliver intelligible speech or use a dialogue box.

Anti-Irys uses the planetary AI’s established dialogue signature.

Anti-Irys does not develop a separate independent voice identity.

Only the following entities use actual dialogue boxes:

- Irys;
- Ion;
- the planetary AI.

## Environmental Sound

Environmental audio should establish scale and activity.

Early regions use:

- distant impacts;
- long reverberation;
- low machinery;
- inactive electrical ambience;
- occasional immense structural movement.

Later regions use:

- active assembly;
- coordinated motors;
- maintenance systems;
- repeated fabrication processes;
- moving transit;
- precise responsive machinery.

The control center should sound busy and fully operational without masking combat.

The shutdown return should audibly remove coordination and leave individual systems failing independently.

## Run Failure and Return

Run failure should sound somber and intimate without treating every failed substrate as a unique melodramatic death.

The sequence should preserve the sense that Irys and Ion have repeated this cycle many times.

A suitable structure is:

1. The current substrate’s internal light and mechanical sound fail abruptly.
2. Active combat layers fall away without forcing complete silence.
3. Ion rises with its persistent tonal signature.
4. The return carries a stripped-down form of the recurring melodic hook.
5. Arrival at the Ark restores Irys’s warmer harmonic identity.

Avoid:

- defeat fanfares;
- triumphant reset jingles;
- exaggerated tragedy;
- generic arcade failure sounds;
- long audio sequences that become tedious across repeated attempts.

The sequence should communicate consequence, continuity, and restrained familiarity.

## Ark Finale

The Ark pod chamber should sound alive during the final duel.

Scripted Ark interventions require audible identities for:

- barriers rising;
- shutters closing;
- pods retracting;
- tracks moving;
- damaged partitions locking;
- the Ark containing the planetary-AI blade.

These sounds should feel protective rather than hostile.

When Irys prevents the planetary AI from escaping, the containment sound should combine:

- the Ark’s warm signature;
- the recurring melodic hook;
- a decisive mechanical lock or field closure.

Ion’s killing blow should remain distinct and final.

## Off-Screen Audio

Off-screen enemy and environmental sound functions primarily as atmosphere.

Directional ambience may suggest:

- distant machinery;
- nearby enemy activity;
- movement beyond the visible route;
- large systems operating elsewhere in the structure.

The player must not require headphones or precise off-screen localization to avoid major attacks.

Combat-critical threats must remain visibly telegraphed within the readable play space.

Environmental machinery should not routinely imitate Technique or enemy telegraph sounds closely enough to create confusion.

Headphones may improve immersion.

They must not provide a required combat advantage.

## Mix Priorities

During active combat, the mix priority is:

1. Player Technique state and activation.
2. Enemy and boss telegraphs.
3. Immediate impacts and damage.
4. Essential environmental hazards.
5. Dialogue.
6. Music.
7. Background machinery and ambience.

Music may be intense, but it must leave space for combat information.

Low-frequency effects should be controlled so repeated Techniques do not produce continuous mud.

Dialogue tones must remain audible without muting the entire soundscape.

## Dynamic Behavior

Music and ambience may change at:

- room transitions;
- boss introductions;
- phase transitions;
- control-center transfer;
- shutdown return;
- final Ark arrival;
- ending transitions.

Avoid constant reactive musical switching tied to ordinary attacks.

Major transitions should be authored and musically deliberate.

Within ordinary stages, dynamic music should use a small number of layered states such as:

- exploration;
- encounter tension;
- active combat;
- post-combat release.

Transitions between those states should preserve the current musical position where practical rather than restarting the composition.

The soundtrack should not become silent during ordinary state changes.

Complete silence remains an exceptional authored dramatic tool.

## Implementation Standard

The initial implementation must include coherent authored audio identities for:

- Ark ambience;
- megastructure ambience;
- continuous low-intensity stage music states;
- restrained reactive exploration and combat layers;
- each Technique;
- Prime activation, persistence, consumption, and expiry;
- the shared Irys–Ion harmonic foundation;
- subtle generated-body mechanical variation;
- restrained synthetic Irys exertion;
- player damage;
- enemy damage;
- each enemy lineage;
- each boss;
- proto-Irys almost-human synthetic vocalization;
- dialogue speaker signatures;
- boss introductions;
- the control-center transfer;
- the shutdown return;
- run-failure and Ion-return audio;
- Ark protection during the final duel;
- the planetary-AI blade’s attempted escape;
- Ion’s final killing blow;
- atmospheric rather than combat-required off-screen sound.

Temporary silence is preferable to mismatched generic sound-library effects that contradict the game’s identity.

However, ordinary gameplay should return to or retain an appropriate musical bed rather than remaining silent.

The Technique tonal relationship should be attempted but may be simplified or removed if it harms clarity or sounds contrived.

## Scope Boundaries

The Audio Direction does not require:

- conventional voice acting;
- intelligible text-to-speech;
- a large adaptive-music middleware system;
- attack-by-attack musical synchronization;
- procedural music generation;
- unique dialogue tones for every generated Irys body;
- realistic gore audio;
- a full orchestral score outside the final confrontation;
- dense or high-intensity music in every room;
- a large granular adaptive-music system;
- exact musical response to every combat input;
- mandatory musical pitch relationships when they reduce Technique clarity;
- fully unique sound sets for every generated body;
- frequent human-like Irys vocalization;
- required headphone use;
- off-screen audio as mandatory attack-warning information.

Additional audio systems should be introduced only when their absence would materially reduce combat readability, environmental progression, narrative clarity, or the emotional impact of the finale.
